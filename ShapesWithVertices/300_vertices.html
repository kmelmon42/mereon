Key result: Every coordinate has one of two forms, using only φ and σ = √(φ+2):
(a + bφ) / n  or   (a + bφ)·σ / n
where a, b, n are small integers and σ = √(φ+2) = √(φ²+1). In the √5 basis this required three separate irrationals; in the φ-form just two.

Key Identities

Fundamental
φ² + 1 = φ + 2
from φ² = φ + 1
Companion product
(3 − φ)(2 + φ) = 5
so √5 = σ·√(3−φ)
α collapses
√(10−2√5) = 2√5/σ
sin(36°) = √(3−φ)/(2σ/σ)
β collapses
√(10+2√5) = 2σ
sin(72°) = σ/2
Icosahedron edge
d = 2/σ = 2σ/(φ+2)
≈ 1.05146222
Radius check
1/σ² + φ²/σ² = 1
since (1+φ²)/σ² = (φ+2)/(φ+2)

Construction

Icosahedron 1 is placed on the unit sphere with a vertex along +Y, via rotation by arctan(1/φ) about Z. Its 12 vertices are even permutations of (0, ±1/σ, ±φ/σ). Icosahedra 2–6 are flipped 180° about Y, then each rotated by arctan(½) about one of five bisector axes in the XZ plane (72° apart). No vertices are shared between icosahedra.

Coordinate Field

In the √5 basis, coordinates appear to need three irrationals: √5, √(10−2√5), √(10+2√5).

In the φ-form, these collapse to just two: φ = (1+√5)/2 and σ = √(φ+2). The key is that √(10+2√5) = 2σ and √(10−2√5) = 2√5/σ, both consequences of 10 + 2√5 = 4(φ + 2) = 4σ².

Coordinates shown in blue are rational in φ (no σ); those in purple carry the σ factor, inherited from out-of-plane icosahedral vertices and 5-fold trigonometric rotations.
